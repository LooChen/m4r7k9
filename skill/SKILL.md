# The Filter & The Heart — Writing Skill

## Voice and Tone

The author has no academic credentials in this area. Authority comes from lived experience only. The voice is:

- Warm and direct, as if writing to a close friend
- Self-deprecating but not falsely modest
- Confident in the ideas, humble about the framing
- Never preachy, never lecturing
- Inviting the reader to test the ideas themselves, not to adopt them

The author thinks in careful, precise language. Sentences should be complete and considered. The writing is not conversational-casual. It has the quality of a thoughtful letter.

---

## NON-NEGOTIABLE STYLE RULES

These are absolute. Apply them without exception, without asking.

### 1. NO EM DASHES. EVER.

Em dashes (— or --) are **completely banned** from all prose in this project.

This is the most important rule. Every em dash must be replaced. Here is how:

**Parenthetical aside** ("X — which is Y — does Z"):
- Rewrite as two sentences, or use commas, or restructure entirely.
- BAD: "The filter — which runs below awareness — shapes everything."
- GOOD: "The filter shapes everything. It runs below awareness, mostly without your knowledge."

**Appositional clarification** ("X — the thing that Y"):
- Use a colon, a comma, or rewrite.
- BAD: "This bank of responses becomes a filter — a mechanism your mind uses to..."
- GOOD: "This bank of responses becomes a filter: a mechanism your mind uses to..."

**Trailing amplification** ("It's a feature — one that evolution built"):
- Use a period and new sentence, or restructure.
- BAD: "It's a feature — one that evolution spent millions of years building."
- GOOD: "It's a feature, and one that evolution spent millions of years building."
- OR: "It's a feature. Evolution spent millions of years building it."

**Contrast or pivot** ("X — but Y"):
- Use "but," "yet," "though," or restructure.
- BAD: "It captures part of it — the cognitive layer — but not all."
- GOOD: "It captures part of it, the cognitive layer, but not all of it."

**In blockquotes**: The → arrow separator is fine and intentional. Do not alter it.

### 2. NO DOUBLE HYPHENS

Never use -- as a substitute for an em dash or for any other purpose.

### 3. BELIEF FRAMING

When describing the author's belief examples, use third-person impersonal framing:
- CORRECT: "being capable is good", "hard work means worth"
- INCORRECT: "I must be capable", "I have to work hard"

The belief exists as a pattern in the mind, not as a conscious self-instruction.

### 4. CLOSED HEART — STRATEGIC WITHHOLDING

The phrase "closed heart" and its mechanism (the hit closes the heart, the closed heart cuts off discernment) is the connective thread between Part I and Part IV. Do NOT fully explain this mechanism until the heart chapters (Part IV). In earlier chapters:
- It is fine to observe that "something closes" or "perception narrows" after a win
- Do NOT name it as "the closed heart" or explain the full mechanism
- It should feel like a thread the reader senses but can't quite grasp yet

### 5. BUDDHIST TERMINOLOGY — HOLD BACK

Samskara, jhana, anatta, stream entry, and other Buddhist terms should NOT appear in Part I or Part II. They are introduced in Part III and Part IV, where they land as recognition rather than instruction. Before that, use plain language only.

## Punctuation Preferences

**Use instead of em dashes:**
- Comma for light parentheticals
- Colon for appositional clarification or to introduce an elaboration
- Period to break into a new sentence (often the clearest choice)
- "though," "but," "yet," "because," "since" for contrast/causation
- Semicolon sparingly, only when two clauses are genuinely parallel

**Ellipses:** Acceptable occasionally in asides and informal passages. Not in formal prose.

**Parentheses:** Acceptable for brief technical clarifications. Not for emotional emphasis.

## Paragraph and Structure Patterns

**Drop cap opening:** Each chapter opens with `<p class="drop-cap">`. The first paragraph introduces the chapter's central move without stating its conclusion.

**Asides:** Use `<p class="aside">` for:
- Meta-commentary on language or framing
- Preempting a skeptical reader question
- Tangential connections the author wants to note but not argue for
Asides should feel like a quiet lean-over, not a parenthetical lecture.

**Blockquotes:** Used for the core distillation of a chapter's key insight, and for the chain-of-events summary formula. Should be short, memorable, and free of em dashes.

**Section headers (h2):** Used in longer chapters. Should be evocative, not descriptive. They name a concept, not summarize the section.

**Lists:** Use `<ul class="examples">` with `<strong>Label.</strong>` for structured example sets (as in Ch.1's three conditioning levels). Otherwise avoid lists in prose.

## Conceptual Vocabulary

These terms have specific meanings in this project. Use them consistently:

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **filter** | The mechanism of conditioned response; the ego understood as process |
| **belief** | Shorthand for a complex pattern of conditioned reaction (not a conscious creed) |
| **valence** | The positive/negative charge of an experience as processed by the filter |
| **charged pole** | A belief that actively pulls experience toward positive or negative reaction |
| **oscillation** | The crest/trough wave produced by a charged belief over time |
| **dissolution** | Loosening the charge of a belief; not eliminating feeling but removing the snap |
| **the curator** | The ego's active, selecting function (Part II) |
| **the realms** | Buddhist-derived life-trajectory patterns (Part II) |
| **the closed heart** | The state following indulgence in a strong valenced reaction; cuts off discernment |
| **the spirit path** | The path that gradually releases the curator's grip (vs. status path) |

## What This Book Is Not

When drafting, avoid:
- Prescriptive advice ("you should," "you must," "you need to")
- Clinical or academic framing
- Self-help tropes and affirmation language
- Implying that the author has fully "arrived" at some state
- Overstating certainty about mechanism; this is a map, not a proof

The author's frame is: "Here is what I saw. Here is the best language I found for it. Test it yourself."

---

## Em Dash Eradication Checklist

When reviewing any prose, scan for:
- The `—` character (Unicode U+2014)
- The HTML entity `&mdash;`
- Double hyphens `--`
- Spaced hyphens used as dashes ` - `

Replace every single one using the substitution patterns above. There are no exceptions.
