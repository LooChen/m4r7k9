Part I — The Machine · Chapter 1

The Ego as Process

75% complete

Most people think of the ego as some beast inside the brain that emerges at inconvenient moments and takes up space. But rather than thinking of the ego as a thing, let’s dig into the actual process of our experience and see how the concept of “ego” might naturally emerge from that process.

The universe is a moving no-thing-ness, with causal activities continuously unfolding. You, as a human, have senses that take in data from that process, and your mind tries to make sense of what it receives. As the result of evolution, culture, and personal experience, you’ve built up a set of reactions, thoughts, and beliefs about these otherwise neutral events that color your experience of the world.

There isn’t a good word I’ve found that captures the whole of these stored “things”. In reality it’s a complex network and process that is always changing. For now, I’m just going to use the word “belief” as a pointer concept for the sake of communication.

In a sense, your collection of beliefs becomes a filter — a mechanism your mind uses to break apart the continuous process of reality into discrete experiences, each with their own flavor, or valence. Broadly speaking, we can bucket those experienced reactions as good or bad, positive or negative.

To see how this works, it helps to look at a few examples. These are deliberate oversimplifications, but they’re enough to illustrate the chain of events:

  • Childhood and culture have solidified the belief that being hard-working is good, and that having this trait makes you a good person. So when your boss praises you in front of your coworkers, you have a pleasant sensation in your abdomen and chest that you might describe as pride.
  • Several years ago, while riding your bike past a large dog, the dog was startled and reactively bit you. Now when you ride by a dog, there’s an immediate recoil upon seeing it, coupled with the acknowledgment of the fear and the need to steer clear. The initial appearance of fear is unpleasant, but the valence turns neutral as you recognize it for what it is.
  • Walking in the woods, the shape of a snake catches your eye — and before you can even form a mental thought, your body clenches and jumps back. A jolt through the whole body, a rush of adrenaline. No belief consulted, no deliberation. Just reaction.

These beliefs or reactions exist differently in your brain, and were created in different ways, but there is a shared order of operations among them:

Events unfold → your mind objectifies and conceptualizes the events → your conditioning judges these events → a reaction arises.

This chain is continuously running as you interact with the neutral process that we call “the universe” breaking events into experiences each with feelings and subsequent narratives that go on to create the story of your life. The filter isn’t bad or good. It’s a feature — one that evolution spent millions of years building. The question is what happens when you start to see it operating.
